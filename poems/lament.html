Lament

The happy days? They've come and gone, consumed
by time. Forgotten memories can haunt
a mind more than the ones recalled, but scars
will help remind me that our past is real,
or so my daughter says while dusting shelves,
arranging flowers by the bed. The smell
about the room: her mother did the same
for me. She loved to garden in the back.
I hate remembering how I forget:

few vague pieced memories remain. I still
recall some tanned and freckled shoulders, bare.
Our scars remind us that our past is real,
but what is real when you no longer know?
Reality becomes pressed leaves; a smell
of roses in the room; old photographs
a woman dusts, now faded by the sun.
The daughter planted roses round the house
today. She cried each time I asked her why.
I hate remembering how I forget

the song she sang aloud when knowing no
one else was near enough to hear the lifting voice.
A smell? The smell of roses stained her hands
and soil mixed in the smell. Sweet earth. A scar.
our scars remind us that our past is real,
so someone said. The smell is real and this:
the dirty black underneath her fingernails.
A dirty smell of roses stained her hands;
I think she loved to garden in the back:
I hate remembering how we forget.

Reprinted from Yemassee Fall/Winter, 1994.
